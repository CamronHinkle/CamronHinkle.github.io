Where do we see this power come into play?


What is the goal?

This major turning ushered in new methods of enforcing power, leaving behind the panoptic institutions that controlled large populations of the past in favor of a new synoptic model where the many watch the few(Nealson, 2012 p. 130). The data from the behavioral surplus enables the automation of population-wide monitoring by just a handful of programs. In these systems, the threat of rebellion is reduced as the watchers willingly impose the rules on themselves and those around them. This is once again extended further by controlling the preferences of individuals through controlling what builds their (endogenous)preferences, the media, and advertising. For example, we can look at the 1998 movie “The Truman Show” where the main character unknowingly lives his entire life from birth on camera for the entertainment of others. This highlights the pervasive influence media has as surveillance not only being able to shape the behavior of the one in front of the camera but also the rest of the population viewing this pseudo-reality show.

This immense power is wielded almost exclusively by unregulated international media conglomerates that have slowly implemented vertical integration to corner the market on our perception. Through their control of the media, they are able to reproduce a skewed and censored worldview with limited interpretations and inaccurate representations. This perpetuates harmful biases as viewers internalize the portals they see as “A key problem with racism is that it can be a self-fulfilling phenomenon if people believe that Joseph will be a less productive worker because of his race there will be a discrimination in hiring him facing lower demand for his services. It is then rational for Joseph to underinvest in his own education because the expected returns will be lower. “(Wright, 141-142). This underscores how we situate ourselves within the context of our environment and if that context continuously tells us what we are like or should be like then we will believe it and replicate it.

In particular, Disney stands out as a cornerstone of our global culture due to the fact that "Disney stories the stories Disney tells will be the stories that will form and help form a child's imaginary world all over the world and that's an incredible amount of power"(Mickey Mouse Monopoly, 2002). Given that we established the sheer amount of time individuals spend consuming media it is important to consider the messages these stories/images tell us about the world. We can see an example of this through how Disney standardized play with their toys. By bombarding individuals with media surrounding a film/story and using each of these as an advertisement it reinforces the messages conveyed. The impact of this can be seen by the fixed nature of play not evolving or changing beyond the plot explored on screen. Play is a form of development and a way to explore the world around them through this they develop skills.

However, their influence does not end with play to standardize how we consume media, form relationships, and work through their brand image. We replicate the lives and relationships we consume even including the corporate brand personalities. Sociologist Erving Goffman described how we view these idealized fictitious worlds as “commercial realism" and these near-perfect worlds could come to fruition through our actions and purchases(746). For instance, many adults consume dating shows, reality TV, or other dramatized narratives where on-screen relationships are depicted as a necessity, not an option. This is reflected in modern dating culture through the stigmatization of being single. Individuals would rather settle for the most reasonable of limited imperfect options rather than embracing solitude or waiting for a genuine connection to naturally form.

Societal pressures drive us to conform yet paradoxically we all strive to stand out and thus converge to form the aesthetic of individuality. As Max Horkheimer and Theodor Adorno eloquently state in Dialectic of Enlightenment “Capitalism promises a system of raw individuality and transcendence of self-being able to act as a free agent in your pursuit of happiness”. However, in practice, this promise is not achieved not only do we share the desire to construct a distinct image of ourselves but we are united in our forced uniformity. By largely consuming the same products due to limited viable options, many similar preferences emerge in the population. Then there is the “or else” mindset of fear and conformity that keeps individuals to norms and expectations. This can be seen when choosing products, defining yourself, or making career decisions leaving many working the same comfortable jobs and spending all they earn on simple needs. Deviation is met with repercussions that limit autonomy for example, you better like what they like or else no one will like you. You better fit into their description or else you won't get hired. You better behave how they want or else you'll get fired. You better work when they want you to or else you won't make rent.

You are promised freedom but have restrictions and challenges imposed to minimize the number of those who deviate. In our late-capitalistic system, this false autonomy can be seen in modern advances like streaming services. They grant you the freedom of choice at the cost of increased individual fees. You pay the price to watch what you want when you want it but end up needing to subscribe to numerous services costing you more. In a way, they are to media as cars are to transportation. Both make you pay many individual fees to increase your autonomy across a preset network of options.
