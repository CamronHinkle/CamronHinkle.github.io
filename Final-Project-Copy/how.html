How Does This System Change the Structure of Feeling?


How Branding Has Changed

With no end to capitalism it can be said that we are experiencing a “slow cancelation of the future”(Bennett, 2022) our future is simply a reinterpretation of the past which changes how we experience time itself. We segment history into distinct epochs by the forms of art and artistic expression associated with them. Selected elements of a period that are still being studied and expressed form a dynamic visual identity or as Gen Z would call it an aesthetic(53-55). Youth cultures of today merely merge these aesthetics to create new combinations that more closely match their visual image without yielding anything profoundly new. This image begins to form the base of our personal brand as an outward projection of the self.

Out of this practice, Lifestyle Branding emerged, and the market reflected this practice in a realization that “to be truly successful …[companies]had to understand that their true product was not their product… it was an idea, a lifestyle, meaning itself” (Klein, 2003). Over time the connection grows stronger as the youth's style and identity become a reflection of the brand's ideologies further solidifying their cultural relevance. We can see this even in the invention of the smartphone where IBM made the first touchscreen phone in 1992 it had a calculator, email, and could even access the web but only 50,000 people bought one(IBM). This was an innovation, a disruption in the technology but it was still just a product with no meaning or style behind it, those with it were most likely labeled a “nerd”. Fifteen years later Apple came along and made functionally the same product but they didn't sell a phone they sold the idea of challenging the status quo, the idea of thinking differently. They did this through beautifully designed, user-friendly products and this just happens to culminate in the iPhone. By selling this idea they changed the word achieving peak market saturation at over 6.1 million phones sold(Sinek, 2009). This shows that we don't want radical new products or forms of art but rather a slow dispersion of existing ideas in optimized versions that resonate with our values.

Along the same lines, if individuals will spend money to hire a product to solve a problem then all the media has to do is convince us that we have a problem and show us a convenient solution. When these problems are created they can be referred to as endogenous preferences or preferences that are created by the economic system itself. Thus “It is circular to argue that markets perform a valuable function by satisfying preferences if the market itself creates them.”(Wright, 104). But the true “triumph of advertising in the culture industry is that consumers feel compelled to buy and use its products even though they see through them.”(Adorno, Bennett, 2022). We learn the practices of international mega-corporations yet we still support them. We understand the harm producing these wasteful products has on the environment yet we still continue to shop. We see the contradictions of capitalism yet we still consume. In this late capitalistic society, production is no longer the goal but rather consumption.

Life Online

Even the simplest situations have become commodified including relaxing at home. We used to have the home as our first place, work as our second, and a third place to relax and socialize. However, these have all more or less been consumed by the digital abstract world. Many people work exclusively online or utilize digital products before coming how to consume digital media to relax, and are only a part of digital communities. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, “Men spent 5.6 hours per day in leisure and sports activities, women 4.9 hours, in 2021” and of that time on average 3.7 hours(66-75%) was spent consuming media(BLS, 2022). This leaves less than 2 hours a day when people are being active, socializing, traveling, or just thinking.

With such a large percentage of our free time spent relaxing in this way, many influencers have emerged and filled this opening in the market now being dubbed “sleepy girls” online. In a culture where mental health awareness is on the rise these creators' bold documentation of their lifestyle opened up an opportunity to profit off of their daily challenges and turned “sadness and struggle into a brand” gaining a sizable market share(Balao, 2023). Journalist Mayi Hughes explores these “mentally unwell, sad girl, sleepy girl archetype influencers” calling them out on building a following based on presenting themselves as “raw, unapologetically “authentic” selves” before launching their own brands and paid podcasts. They have reached a new level of the social interpretation of culture where depicting everyday life is considered culture. This capitalizes on the public's desire to consume content and fit in. However, the danger is in the fact that these individuals are not being raw and authentic, it is glamorized and edited which in turn leads to a boom in average individuals expressing their own personal brand through forced “candid” images and photo dumps where people feel the need to stage photo ops. Our digital presence has become an edited choreographed facade more about the idea of how you appear or are represented to others than how you actually are.

These social networking sites are publicly available and contribute to how others perceive you. In our late capitalistic society, these networks become the main channel for sustaining relationships without physical interactions. However, as we previously established we spend so much time pursuing connections around the world and consuming what they produce that we begin to have less of a stake in each of these relationships. Consequently, the average number of close friends has decreased with only 13% of people having 10 or more in 2021 compared to 33% in 1990. Additionally, in 1990 only 3% of responders indicated that they had no close friends which has since increased to 12%(Majidi, 2023). In contrast as of 2023, the average number of Instagram followers per user was over 150. All of these networking sites and still we are becoming more disconnected. Studies have identified networking to be the predominant reason for utilizing social media(Lefika and Tracy, 2020). We collect and cultivate connections but only reap a fraction of the benefits.

The Personal is Political

Our desire to network and our preference to consume new media combine to create the rising habit of over-consuming digital media. This preference itself is not of your own choosing; it has been taken advantage of and marketed to the extreme allowing the line to become blurred between personal and professional in many jobs, particularly those in the digital realm. Some insist that you complete personal projects to gain work experience before entering the field. These projects are then compiled into another form of personal branding through the creation of a portfolio website showcasing your collection of creative work. Portfolios also reflect and reinforce the values of the capitalistic system, wherein one's worth is often measured by their ability to generate profitable products or services. By having targeted recommendations are sorted to the top it gives prospective employees an incentive to lie or stretch the truth to get where they want. This is known and openly recommended rather than just making the expectations more realistic. Thus, keeping constant control over your digital footprint gives you a more cohesive personal brand further enforcing the cycle.

Creating a strong personal brand starts with self-discovery. You have to know yourself before you can effectively project yourself to the world…and communicates your value to those you are seeking to do business with.”(Blacksmith, Poeppelman, 113-114). We have age restrictions on the use of alcohol, drugs, gambling, and other highly addictive coping mechanisms yet we willingly hand younger and younger children unfettered control over limitless media. Perpetuating this cycle will only further destabilize this unhealthy behavior. This internalization of personal branding is entwined with the mantra "the personal is political" where we can see the micro world of interactions and individual experiences being tied to the larger social structures and power dynamics at play. (Hanisch, 1969). This blurring is exacerbated by the solipsistic shift of personal issues as a problem for the whole world, using social media to share your emotions.

Conversely, the shifting of global or corporate problems onto the individual. For instance, in the problem of climate change, large industries contribute a significant percentage of the emissions worldwide(Statista, 2024). Yet, their response often revolves around marketing products as more "sustainable" and encouraging climate-friendly alternatives rather than implementing those changes at their level. The top three reasons why European companies invest in sustainable marketing strategies were: For the brand image(20.76%), To increase sales(18.26%), and then because it's the right thing to do(13.93%)(Majidi, 2024). If we simplify the 9 categories it becomes 82.16% to make more money and 22.18% for the environment itself demonstrating capitalism's preference to maximize profit above all else. Furthermore, they may simply be purchasing "Carbon offsets" that don't necessarily ensure carbon is removed let alone they then no longer have to have the moral weight of cultural destruction on their shoulders, yet they still gain the same sales benefits.

Survalience Capitalism

Throughout history, information has always been a form of currency traded to gain insight into others around you but now more than ever it has become the new gold. This contemporary stage of capitalism hinges on data collection with corporations vying to gather large amounts of information allowing them to fuel targeted advertising and enabling more accurate algorithms. Shoshana Zuboff explored this idea dubbing it Survalience capitalism saying "It is no longer enough to automate information flows about us; the goal is to automate us."(Zuboff, 2018). Tech giants like Apple and Google exemplify these ideas as they continue to highlight the importance of privacy but only when it is closed-source and proprietary. They then expand their scope to extract more and more insights from your behavior. Zuboff goes on to point out that“The pattern of violations is a feature, not a bug”.

Surveillance capitalism does not just stem from corporations but rather from their symbiotic relationship with governments to aid each other in the pursuit of information gathering. Governments make claims of the data's use for national security but are willing to encroach upon privacy rights to do so. For example, the UK has attempted to ban end-to-end encryption(E2EE) as it hinders law enforcement efforts(Gov.uk, 2023). Or even the recent ban on TikTok for “data safety” when it is simply corruption. Despite these efforts, no coercion is required as we willingly submit to being tracked, recorded, and monitored in various ways. Whether we consent through lengthy end-user license agreements or simply by what we provide to others in our personal branding efforts and larger digital footprint, we are complicit in the proliferation of our own data. As Ian Bennett of Epoch philosophy aptly states, it becomes a "narcissistic and parasocial endeavor of bastardized image-based friendships and self-consumption." Thus, capitalism has become even more abstracted from its original intention with a prioritization for digital data over concrete goods and services.
