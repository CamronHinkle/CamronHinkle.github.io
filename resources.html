Resources


What is the goal?

With no end to capitalism it can be said that we are experiencing a “slow cancelation of the future”(Bennett, 2022) our future is simply a reinterpretation of the past which changes how we experience time itself. We segment history into distinct epochs by the forms of art and artistic expression associated with them. Each of these is what Raymond Williams refers to in The Analysis of Culture as “the culture of selected tradition” that consists of the selected elements of a period that are still being studied and expressed forming a dynamic visual identity or as Gen Z would call it an aesthetic(53-55). Youth cultures of today merely merge these aesthetics to create new combinations that more closely match their visual image without yielding anything profoundly new. This image begins to form the base of our personal brand as an outward projection of the self.

Out of this practice, Lifestyle Branding emerged, and the market reflected this practice in a realization that “If companies wanted to be truly successful and competitive in the global marketplace they had to understand that their true product was not their product… it was an idea, a lifestyle, meaning itself” (Klein, 2003). Through this process brands form a symbiotic relationship where they become synonymous with the lifestyle of their target demographic. When each of these market segments identifies with a style they like this path guides them straight to the connected brand. The connection only grows stronger as the youth's style and identity become a reflection of the brand's ideologies further solidifying their cultural relevance. It didn't matter what the brand did, but rather why they did it. It matters what a consumer is thinking or experiencing while using a product and even more so how others will perceive them in relation to the products they have. IBM made the first touchscreen phone in 1992 it had a calculator, email, and could even access the web but only 50,000 people bought one(IBM). This was an innovation, a disruption in the technology but it was still just a product with no meaning or style behind it, those with it were most likely labeled a “nerd”. Fifteen years later Apple came along and made functionally the same product but they didn't sell a phone they sold the idea of challenging the status quo, the idea of thinking differently. They did this through beautifully designed, user-friendly products and this just happens to culminate in the iPhone. By selling this idea they changed the word achieving peak market saturation at over 6.1 million phones sold(Sinek, 2009). This shows that we don't want radical new products or forms of art but rather a slow dispersion of existing ideas in optimized versions that resonate with our values.

When we are in any situation there are always motivations to do something to achieve our desired outcome. If we're in a time crunch then we have a motivation to grab a quick bite to eat to satisfy that hunger. Clayton Christensen dubbed this the jobs-to-be-done method: when you need a task or job done, you essentially hire a product to do that job. He said, “If a [businessperson] can understand the job, design a product and associated experiences…then when customers find themselves needing to get that job done they will hire that product”(The Fullstory Team, 2020). In lamens terms to get more people to consume your product you need to understand the why behind the what. His favorite example was that people on their morning commute hire a milkshake to fill them up and keep them entertained while driving. This user-centered approach further shifted lifestyle branding as marketing didn't need to improve the product they just had to target the specific scenarios their customers needed a job to be solved. Gone were the days of buying luxury products for their quality alone if you only wanted to hire expensive clothes to buy into their brand image. It doesn't matter what the product is just that it improves your personal brand image. For most corporations, the only job to be done is to make more money. So if a simple t-shirt with their logo will satisfy the customer's desires and save them money it is a mutually beneficial trade. Furthermore, this makes it much easier to rehire an old product than to invest in making a new one that might do the job. If a movie studio made a cult classic why invest to search for the next classic if they can invest the same amount to repackage the original movie? Disney remade their classic Cinderella movie in 2015 costing $95 million but grossing over $542.4 million worldwide, a classic story with dozens of movie adaptations and hundreds of variations yet it is still widely loved so why not satisfy this preference?

Along the same lines, if individuals will spend money to hire a product to solve a problem then all the media has to do is convince us that we have a problem and show us a convenient solution. When these problems are created they can be referred to as endogenous preferences or preferences that are created by the economic system itself(Wright, 104). The question then becomes how many of your preferences are truly your own? Even preferences for your favorite foods cannot be wholly attributed to you as we have specific palettes that corporations can target. Between your biological programmed reward pathways and the media, nearly all preferences from politics to pop culture can be explained. Thus “It is circular to argue that markets perform a valuable function by satisfying preferences if the market itself creates them.”(Wright, 104) our preferences are polluted by the market. But the true “triumph of advertising in the culture industry is that consumers feel compelled to buy and use its products even though they see through them.”(Adorno, Bennett, 2022). We learn the practices of international mega-corporations yet we still support them. We understand the harm producing these wasteful products has on the environment yet still we continue to shop. We see the contradictions of capitalism yet still we consume. In this late capitalistic society, production is no longer the goal but rather consumption.

Even the simplest situations have become commodified including relaxing at home. Relaxing in your private space was once an escape from the hustle and bustle of the outside world but it has become just another environment to consume products and media. Now when many people think about relaxing they want to put on their comfy clothes, which are branded from Lululemon to Nike, and then destress by scrolling social media or binge-watching shows. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, “Men spent 5.6 hours per day in leisure and sports activities, women 4.9 hours, in 2021” and of that time on average 3.7 hours(66-75%) was spent consuming media(BLS, 2022). This leaves less than 2 hours a day when people are being active, socializing, traveling, or just thinking.

With such a large percentage of our free time spent relaxing in this way, many influencers have emerged and filled this opening in the market now being dubbed “sleepy girls” online. Through Raymond Williams's social definition of culture, found in “The Analysis of Culture”, we can see how these influencers are able to depict “a particular way of life” or “ordinary behaviors” in a new way(48). In a culture where mental health awareness is on the rise these creators' bold documentation of their lifestyle opened up an opportunity to profit off of their daily challenges and turned “sadness and struggle into a brand” gaining a sizable market share(Balao, 2023). Journalist Mayi Hughes, a Harvard Graduate with a background in sociology and politics, took to TikTok in an attempt to raise awareness of these branded personalities. She explores these “mentally unwell, sad girl, sleepy girl archetype influencers” calling them out on building a following based on presenting themselves as “raw, unapologetically “authentic” selves” before launching their own brands and paid podcasts. Some of the most popular of these lazy influencers include Emma Chamberlin and Madeline Argy with millions of followers each. They put in countless hours of work to appear effortless showcasing the reality of their life. These vloggers led to a boom in average individuals expressing their own personal brand through forced “candid” images and photo dumps where people feel the need to stage photo ops.
